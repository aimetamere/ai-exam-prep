M1 · Data Foundations
Internal vs External Data Sources — Where Does Your Data Come From?
Most organisations overlook 60–70% of available data. Source awareness is the first step to understanding value and risk.
🏢 Internal Data Sources
Data generated by your own operations. Highest trust, easiest governance, often the most competitive.

Transactional: ERP, POS, billing, payments — the operational heartbeat.
Customer: CRM, support tickets, emails, call recordings, NPS surveys.
Product: App events, clickstreams, feature usage, session logs.
People: HR databases, time tracking (strict GDPR obligations).
Financial: Ledgers, forecasts, budget actuals — most sensitive structured data.
⚠️ The Internal Data Trap
Most companies have abundant internal data stored across 12+ disconnected systems, with 6+ conflicting definitions of key metrics like "revenue" or "active customer." The problem is never volume. It is coherence and ownership.
🌐 External Data Sources
Data acquired from outside the organisation. Powerful for enrichment, benchmarking, and AI training.

Purchased: Bloomberg, Refinitiv, Experian, Dun & Bradstreet, satellite imagery, web scraping.
Open / Public: Eurostat, government statistics, OpenStreetMap, weather APIs, public company filings.
Partnerships: Supplier data sharing, distributor flows — increasingly regulated by the EU Data Act (2024).
Synthetic: AI-generated datasets that mimic real-world distributions — used for AI training without privacy risk.
💰 The External Data Market
€400B+ global data market (2025). Companies like Uber, Mastercard, and Airbnb sell anonymised operational data as a secondary revenue stream. Your transactional data may be worth more to a logistics company or urban planner than you realise.
Data Sensitivity Classification Tiers
🔴 Restricted
Highest sensitivity. Breach = regulatory fine, criminal liability, or material harm. Examples: health/biometric data, passwords, payment card numbers, criminal records, trade secrets. Access: named individuals only, encrypted at rest + in transit, audit every access.
🟠 Confidential
Business-sensitive. Exposure harms competitive position or violates contracts. Examples: PII (names, emails, addresses), salary data, M&A plans, customer contracts, pricing models. Access: role-based, internal only, no external sharing without legal review.
🔵 Internal
General business use. Harmless if shared within organisation; damaging if leaked externally. Examples: operational reports, product roadmaps, KPIs, internal policies, aggregated metrics. Access: all employees; not for public or partner sharing.
🟢 Public
Approved for external release. No harm from public access. Examples: press releases, published annual reports, open API data, marketing content, public-facing pricing. Access: no restrictions; freely shareable.

M2 · Architecture
Accessibility vs Control — The Fundamental Architecture Trade-Off
Every architecture decision is a point on a spectrum between maximum openness and maximum control. Business strategy — not technical preference — determines where you should sit.
🔓 High Accessibility
Data easily findable, queryable, and usable by anyone in the organisation without filing IT requests.

Benefits: Faster decisions, self-service analytics, innovation at the edge, reduced bottleneck.
Risks: PII exposure, inconsistent metrics, shadow analysis, compliance failures.

Architecture signals: Data Mesh, self-serve BI, broad RBAC, open data catalogues.
Suits: Tech companies, data-native organisations.
🔒 High Control
Strict access controls, centralised governance, approval workflows before any data access.

Benefits: GDPR compliance, IP protection, consistent single source of truth, audit trail.
Risks: Bottlenecks, shadow Excel, slow innovation, talent frustration.

Architecture signals: Central data team as gatekeeper, strict RBAC, row-level security, VPC isolation.
Suits: Financial services, healthcare, government.
The Business Leader's Decision Framework
Ask three questions:
1. What is the regulatory sensitivity of the data? (GDPR Art.9 data = high control required)
2. What is the cost of a wrong decision driven by bad data access? (Credit decisions = high control; marketing copy = high accessibility)
3. What is the cost of being too slow? (Competitive market = accessibility; regulated monopoly = control)

ETL vs ELT — A Philosophical Shift, Not Just an Acronym Swap
Where transformation happens determines who controls business logic, how fast it changes, and what it costs.
❌ ETL — Extract, Transform, Load (Legacy)
Extract data → Transform on a dedicated ETL server → Load clean data into warehouse.

Business logic lives in proprietary IT tools. Analysts file tickets to change a calculation — changes take weeks. Licences cost €200K+/year. Brittle: one broken transformation can halt the entire pipeline.

Business locked out of own logicSlow iterationExpensive
Informatica · SSIS · Talend · IBM DataStage
✅ ELT — Extract, Load, Transform (Modern)
Extract raw data → Load into warehouse raw → Transform inside the warehouse using SQL (via dbt).

Business logic lives in version-controlled SQL files — analysts can read, change, and test it themselves. Raw data always preserved. Changes take hours. 50,000+ companies have adopted this.

Analysts own the logicFast iterationAuditable
dbt · Fivetran · Airbyte · Snowflake
The Business Impact of ELT — Vodafone Case
Vodafone replaced Informatica ETL with dbt + Fivetran: data team productivity up 3×, new analysis deployable in hours instead of weeks, licence costs reduced €400K/year. The analogy: ETL is custom-built plumbing. ELT is connecting to the city water system. The same revolution applies to most large European enterprises still running Informatica.

Batch vs Real-Time Streaming — The Right Speed for the Right Decision
The most common over-engineering mistake: streaming data that doesn't need to be streamed. Real-time costs 5–10× in complexity and money.
🕐 Batch Processing
Data collected → processed at scheduled intervals (hourly, nightly, weekly). Simple, reliable, cost-effective. The right choice for ~90% of business analytics needs.

Tools: Apache Spark, AWS Glue, dbt, Azure Data Factory
Examples: Walmart nightly inventory sync across 10,000 stores, monthly payroll, quarterly financial reports, weekly sales summaries

✅ Simple✅ Cheap✅ Reliable❌ Latency: minutes–hours
⚡ Real-Time Streaming
Data processed event-by-event as it arrives. Sub-second latency. Justified only when a business decision genuinely changes based on seconds-old data.

Tools: Apache Kafka, AWS Kinesis, Apache Flink, Azure Event Hubs
Examples: Uber surge pricing (1T events/day on Kafka), bank fraud detection (<200ms), real-time personalisation

✅ Real-time❌ Complex❌ 5–10× more expensive
⚠️ The Streaming Trap — The Most Expensive Mistake in Data Engineering
Teams default to Kafka because it sounds impressive or "we might need it later." But streaming architecture adds 5–10× complexity and ongoing operational burden. The test: What business decision changes if data is 15 minutes old vs 1 second old? If the answer is "nothing material" — use batch. A dashboard refreshed every 15 minutes serves 95% of BI needs at 5% of the cost.

M4 · Data Strategy
What Is a Data Strategy? — And What It Is Not
Most "data strategies" are technology procurement plans. The best ones are competitive strategy documents that happen to involve data.
❌ What a Data Strategy Is NOT
  • "We will migrate to Snowflake and implement a data lake by Q4" — this is a technology roadmap
  • "We will collect more data" — this is storage procurement
  • "We will hire 10 data scientists" — this is an HR plan without a business question
  • "We will become data-driven" — this is a values statement, not a strategy
  • "We will implement Power BI across all business units" — this is a reporting rollout
✅ What a Data Strategy IS
  • A prioritised set of business decisions that data must enable — with measurable outcomes
  • A data ownership model — who is accountable for quality, freshness, and compliance of each critical dataset
  • A governance framework — how data is classified, protected, and used within legal and ethical boundaries
  • A capability roadmap — skills, infrastructure, and processes needed to execute
  • A value measurement model — how you know if data is creating competitive advantage
The 5-Question Data Strategy Framework
① What decisions must data support? The 5 most valuable decisions currently made with insufficient data.
② What data would improve each decision? Source, type, quality needed, frequency required.
③ Who owns each dataset? Named individual — not "the data team."
④ What are the compliance constraints? GDPR, sector regulations, contractual limits on data use.
⑤ What is the business value of getting this right? Quantify: revenue upside, cost reduction, risk mitigation.
Real Data Strategies — What They Look Like
Amazon: "Every customer decision — what to show, recommend, price — will be data-driven. Every team owns its data products." → Personalisation engine worth $35B+/year in incremental revenue.

ING Bank: "Every business domain owns its data. Central team sets standards only." → Data Mesh rollout, 9 conflicting revenue definitions eliminated, time-to-insight from weeks to hours.

Zalando: "50+ data products across 30 domains, each with an SLA." → 5× increase in data use cases per quarter.
Data as Competitive Advantage — 3 Patterns
  • Data depth: You know your customers better than any competitor — Spotify's listening data, Uber's mobility data
  • Data speed: You make decisions faster — real-time pricing, inventory, fraud — Amazon, Booking.com
  • Data network effects: More users → more data → better product → more users — Google, LinkedIn, Waze

M4 · Data Strategy
Why Data Strategies Fail — 7 Documented Root Causes
85% of data initiatives fail to deliver expected business value. These failures are predictable, documented, and avoidable.
❌ 1. Technology-First
Buying Snowflake before defining use cases. "We need a data lake" is not a strategy. Technology without a business question is infrastructure without purpose.
❌ 2. No Data Ownership
Everyone uses data, nobody maintains it. Central team blamed for quality failures from systems they don't control. ING pre-Mesh: 9 teams, 9 conflicting versions of "customer revenue."
❌ 3. Central Bottleneck
One data team serving 50 business units. Every request: 6 weeks wait. Teams build shadow Excel. 40% of "analysis" at one logistics firm lived in personal spreadsheets.
❌ 4. Stale Data, Real Decisions
Dashboards powered by month-old data presented as current. Decision-makers don't know the latency. Zillow's AI trained on pre-pandemic data — $500M loss.
❌ 5. Compliance Afterthought
GDPR and regulatory requirements discovered after the platform is built. Retrofits cost 4–7× more. The legal team's first data audit finds engineering never designed for compliance.
❌ 6. Vanity Metrics
Measuring data volume, number of dashboards, pipeline count. "We have a data lake!" — with zero business decisions improved. Measure outcomes, not outputs.
❌ 7. No Executive Ownership
Data strategy delegated entirely to the CTO or CDO without CEO/CFO-level sponsorship. Data is a cross-functional discipline — it fails when treated as a department. When the CFO won't change a P&L metric definition "because it's always been that way" — the data strategy is dead on arrival regardless of technology.
✅ What Successful Strategies Have in Common
  • Named business decision as the starting point — not a technology choice
  • Executive sponsor with P&L accountability, not just technical sponsor
  • Named data owners for every critical dataset — not "the data team"
  • Quality SLAs published and monitored — freshness, completeness, accuracy
  • Outcome metrics: decisions improved, not data volume stored
  • Compliance designed in from day one — legal basis before data collection

M6 · Decisions
Data Storytelling for Executives — Turning Numbers Into Decisions
The most sophisticated analysis is worthless if it can't change a decision in a boardroom. Communication is not a soft skill — it is a data skill.
1
Lead with the Insight, Not the Data
Bad: "Slide 1: Data sources. Slide 2: Methodology. Slide 3: Chart. Slide 4: Conclusion." Good: "Revenue is declining 8% MoM in Germany — here is why, and here are three options." Executives buy conclusions, not methodologies. Put the finding on slide 1, the evidence on slide 2.
2
One Chart, One Decision
Every visualisation should answer exactly one question that a specific person needs to make a specific decision. If you can't state the decision in one sentence before showing the chart, the chart is not boardroom-ready.
3
State Uncertainty Explicitly
Data has confidence intervals. Models have accuracy rates. Forecasts have error margins. The most trusted data leaders say "this model is right 80% of the time — here is the 20% scenario." Overconfidence destroys credibility when wrong.
4
Connect Every Insight to a Business Outcome
Bad: "Customer acquisition cost increased 23%." Good: "CAC increased 23%, reducing LTV/CAC from 4.2× to 3.4× — below our 3.5× threshold for sustainable growth. We recommend pausing Channel X spend." The second version cannot be ignored.
The McKinsey Pyramid — For Data Presentations
Start with the governing thought (the recommendation) → support with key arguments (the three reasons) → support each with data. Never reverse this structure for executive audiences. Data should support conclusions — never build up to them.

Template:
• "We should [action] because [finding]."
• "The finding is supported by [data 1], [data 2], [data 3]."
• "The risk of not acting is [quantified outcome]."
⚠️ The Three Data Communication Failures
  • Precision theatre: Reporting to 4 decimal places when data uncertainty is ±20%
  • Chart overload: 12 charts on one slide communicating zero decisions
  • Missing the "so what": Presenting a finding with no recommended action
The Test for Any Data Presentation
"If the CEO saw only this slide, what decision would they make differently?" If you can't answer — rewrite the slide.

Deep Dive · M7 / M10 · Data Contracts
Data Contracts — The Minimum Viable Governance Unit
A data contract is a formal, machine-readable agreement between data producer and consumer. The single most impactful governance artefact a team can implement.
What a Data Contract Contains
Schema: Field names, types, descriptions, required/optional. Breaking changes trigger notifications.
SLA: Freshness ("updated every 15 minutes"), completeness ("max 1% null in required fields"), uptime ("99.5%").
Ownership: Named producer team, named contact, escalation path for quality failures.
Classification: Sensitivity tier, GDPR legal basis, allowed purposes, what AI systems may access it.
Retention: How long data is retained, deletion schedule, archival policy.
Consumers: Which teams consume this product and for what use cases — used for impact analysis.
Quality rules: Specific business rules that must always be true ("price must be > 0", "order status must be in [pending, confirmed, shipped, delivered]").
Why Data Contracts Work — Before vs After
Before: A data engineer changes a column name upstream → 12 dashboards break silently → CFO presents wrong revenue to the board → everyone blames everyone.

With contracts: Column name change triggers automated alert to all consumers → CI/CD pipeline fails if change is breaking → producer must version the schema → consumers have time to adapt.

Standard: ODCS (Open Data Contract Specification) — developed by PayPal. Adopters: Zalando, ING, JP Morgan, Mercado Libre. Tools: Soda Core, Atlan, Datacontract CLI.
Data Contracts as GDPR Evidence
Under GDPR, being able to demonstrate that data has a documented legal basis, purpose, retention policy, and classification is exactly what regulators request during audits. A data contract is your GDPR Art.30 Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) for a specific dataset — in machine-readable form. Companies with data contracts reduce regulatory audit preparation from weeks to hours.

The KPI Design Framework — 5 Rules

  • Named owner: Every KPI must have a person who acts when it moves outside bounds

Deep Dive · M5 · Monetisation Strategy
Building the Data Monetisation Business Case for Your CFO
Turning data into revenue is not a technology project — it requires legal, product, technical, and commercial alignment simultaneously.
1
Start With What You Already Have
Most organisations hold monetisable data they don't recognise as such. Operational data with competitive value: transaction patterns, footfall data, supply chain timings, equipment performance. The question is not "do we have valuable data?" but "to whom is it valuable and what is our legal basis for sharing it?"
2
Legal Basis Before Revenue Model
Before any external data product launch: (1) What GDPR legal basis covers external sharing? (2) Is the data truly anonymous or only pseudonymous? (3) What contractual obligations restrict sharing (supplier contracts, employee data, customer T&Cs)? Get legal sign-off before building. This eliminates 40% of monetisation ideas that looked profitable on a spreadsheet.
3
Choose the Right Revenue Model
Subscription: Fixed recurring access — Bloomberg, Refinitiv. Predictable, requires ongoing quality investment.
Usage-based API: Pay per query — Twilio, Stripe Radar. Scales with customer success.
Insight licensing: Research reports derived from your data — Mastercard SpendingPulse. Lower volume, higher margin.
Data marketplace: List on Snowflake Marketplace. Lower cost to market, lower pricing power.
Data Monetisation Readiness Checklist
  • Legal basis for external sharing confirmed (GDPR + contracts reviewed)
  • Data classified and sensitivity confirmed (truly anonymous, not pseudonymous)
  • Data quality SLA documented and verifiable
  • Re-identification risk assessment completed (MIT 87% test applied)
  • Revenue model and pricing strategy defined
  • Named buyer with intent to pay confirmed — not hypothetical
  • Data product owner assigned post-launch
The Competitive Moat Question
Before monetising externally, ask: "Does selling this data weaken our competitive position?" Your logistics data about competitor shipping times is valuable to your competitors. Monetise to non-competitors in adjacent industries. The best external data customers are companies who can create value with your data that you cannot create yourself.

Classify before you build

Never confuse correlation with causation

Stream only what requires streaming
